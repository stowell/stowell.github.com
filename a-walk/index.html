A Walk

Far down the list of actual problems, if one can even partially order those, is what to listen to. When you're up in the mountains and only have five and a half FM stations it's not that hard. When you have millions of playlists it's harder, especially under time pressure (the start of a short drive, walking just under a mile). I want to pretend for a few paragraphs that this is important and pick apart the mental friction that arises when you have a vague notion of what you want to hear.

Friday morning I wanted some background music for coding. Except it couldn't be what I listened to yesterday afternoon (Mountains's Centralia). Or drone in general. Nothing too quiet. Or apocalyptic. Or crescendoing. I could click on 'Focus' but there's no way I trust other people to focus the same way I do. Nothing too robotic. Nothing from the future. Not guitar-heavy.

I ended up listening to Lush Vibes because I know, trust, and love the people who make it. And I had listened to it for a few minutes the day before and knew it wasn't going to attack me. It was perfect. If I hadn't already known about the playlist though I would've been stuck, with no real way to get from my sense of what I want to hear to actual music. When I give up I listen to something I've heard recently or something in my heavy rotation. Or just take off my headphones and take a walk.

Most people don't have as much of an incentive to use Spotify as I do, so they must give up sooner, probably by closing the app, probably for good.

What I want to do is to take my checklist from above and turn it into a walk through the space of music-that's-probably-good-by-me. It's tempting to pull out all the data we have and try to find a path from, say, my cluster of drone artists to Lush Vibes. Even if we can cover all cases, though, how do you show someone the graph from their heavy rotation to everything just outside it? We've got to give them a fast way to get to where they don't even know they want to go.

The idea I've been stuck on for some time is a screen that has four playlists. You press play on one of the playlists. After you're well into it the other playlists on the screen fade to different playlists that are close to the one you're listening to. If you stop playing the playlist right away or otherwise give it the thumbs down it fades and is replaced by something obviously different.

The real question is whether turning a subconscious checklist into button presses is worth it. The advantage is that people progress through the music by actually listening to it instead of reading/guessing about it. It takes away the part where you translate between a title, an image, and a two-sentence description to a song. The disadvantage is that it's probably not something you're going to do when you're focused on something else.
